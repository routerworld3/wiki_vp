## Historical Context: Why did Advaita Vedānta rise during Śhaṅkarācārya’s time?

Adi Shankaracharya emerged around the **8th century CE** during a very important intellectual and religious transition in India.

At that time, India was not dominated by a single unified “Hindu philosophy.” Instead, there were many competing systems:

* Buddhism
* Jainism
* Ritual-heavy Vedic traditions (Mīmāṃsā)
* Early devotional movements
* Sāṅkhya, Yoga, Nyāya, Vaiśeṣika philosophies
* Tantric traditions
* Materialist schools like Cārvāka

So Śhaṅkara’s work was not happening in a vacuum.

---

# 1) Buddhism’s Influence on Advaita’s Rise

One major reason for Advaita’s rise was the enormous intellectual influence of Buddhism in India at that time.

Especially:

* **Mādhyamika Buddhism** (Nāgārjuna)
* **Yogācāra Buddhism**

These schools emphasized:

* impermanence
* illusion-like experience
* emptiness (śūnyatā)
* the unreliability of ordinary perception
* liberation through knowledge/realization

Śhaṅkara strongly debated Buddhist philosophers.

However, something important happened historically:

## Śhaṅkara borrowed the philosophical rigor, but rejected Buddhist conclusions

### Similarities people noticed

Both Advaita and Buddhism discuss:

* the world as transient
* ignorance causing bondage
* liberation through realization
* ordinary identity being false

Because of this, later critics sometimes called Advaita:

> “Hidden Buddhism” (Prachanna Bauddha)

---

# 2) But Advaita is fundamentally different from Buddhism

Śhaṅkara disagreed with Buddhism on a crucial point.

## Buddhism (especially Madhyamika)

Ultimate reality:

* emptiness (śūnyatā)
* no permanent self (anātman)

## Advaita Vedānta

Ultimate reality:

* eternal consciousness (Brahman)
* true Self exists (Ātman)
* Ātman = Brahman

So:

* Buddhism says ultimately “no enduring self”
* Advaita says ultimate reality is pure eternal consciousness itself

This is the biggest difference.

---

# 3) Another reason Advaita rose: reaction to ritualism

At that time, many Vedic traditions emphasized:

* rituals
* sacrifices
* priestly authority
* karma-kāṇḍa (ritual action)

Śhaṅkara shifted focus toward:

* direct realization
* Upaniṣadic inquiry
* self-knowledge (jñāna)

This was spiritually attractive because it simplified liberation:

> Liberation comes through knowledge of the Self, not merely ritual performance.

---

# 4) Śhaṅkara’s Major Contribution

Śhaṅkara unified:

* Upaniṣads
* Bhagavad Gītā
* Brahma Sūtra

into one coherent non-dual philosophical system.

These three are called:

> **Prasthāna-trayī** (the three foundational Vedānta texts)

He established Advaita as a serious philosophical school.

---

# 5) Śhaṅkara’s Main Philosophical Ideas

## Core Advaita Formula

```text
Brahman alone is ultimately real.
The world is dependent/temporary.
The individual self is not different from Brahman.
```

Often summarized as:

> Brahma satyam
> Jagan mithyā
> Jīvo brahmaiva nāparaḥ

Meaning:

* Brahman is ultimate reality
* the world is conditionally real/transient
* the individual self is Brahman

---

# 6) Why Śhaṅkara became so influential

He did several things:

### A) Wrote commentaries

On:

* Upaniṣads
* Bhagavad Gītā
* Brahma Sūtra

These became foundational.

---

### B) Defeated rival schools in debate

Traditional accounts describe debates with:

* Buddhists
* Mīmāṃsakas
* Sāṅkhyas
* others

---

### C) Established monasteries (Maṭhas)

Traditionally associated with:

* Sringeri
* Dwaraka
* Puri
* Jyotirmath

These helped institutionalize Advaita across India.

---

# 7) Interesting paradox: Śhaṅkara and Bhakti (devotion)

This is where many people get surprised.

Although Śhaṅkara is the greatest Advaita philosopher, he also composed deeply devotional hymns.

This appears contradictory:

* If ultimate reality is formless Brahman,
* then why devotional songs to personal deities?

But Advaita explains this in layers:

* devotion purifies the mind
* personal God (Īśvara) is a valid spiritual reality
* devotion prepares one for non-dual realization

So bhakti was not rejected.

---

# 8) Famous devotional works attributed to Śhaṅkara

## Bhaja Govindam

Very famous bhajan/stotra.

Main theme:

* intellectual pride alone cannot save you
* devotion to Govinda (Kṛṣṇa) is necessary
* worldly knowledge without realization is useless

Famous line:

> Bhaja Govindam, Bhaja Govindam
> Govindam Bhaja Mūḍha Mate

Meaning:

> Worship Govinda, O foolish mind.

This is fascinating because the greatest non-dualist philosopher strongly emphasized devotion.

---

## Nirvana Shatakam

Pure Advaita expression.

Famous line:

> Chidananda Rūpaḥ Śivo’ham Śivo’ham

Meaning:

> I am pure consciousness and bliss; I am Shiva.

This expresses direct non-dual realization.

---

## Saundarya Lahari

A highly devotional and tantric hymn to the Divine Mother.

Shows:

* Śhaṅkara was not merely abstract philosophy
* he integrated devotion, mantra, and worship traditions

---

## Dakshinamurti Stotram

Explains silent transmission of Self-knowledge from guru to disciple.

---

## Shivānanda Lahari

Deeply emotional devotion to Śiva.

Again surprising for people who think Advaita is “dry philosophy.”

---

# 9) The apparent contradiction in Śhaṅkara

This confuses many students:

## Question

If Advaita says:

* ultimately no duality,
* then why worship Kṛṣṇa, Śiva, Devi?

## Advaita answer

Two levels of reality:

* **Vyāvahārika** (practical/devotional level)
* **Pāramārthika** (ultimate non-dual level)

At practical level:

* devotion
* ethics
* worship
* guru
* meditation

are all valid and necessary.

At ultimate realization:

* knower, known, and worshipped become one consciousness.

---

# 10) Historical Impact of Śhaṅkara

Śhaṅkara helped:

* revive Vedic/Upaniṣadic thought
* intellectually challenge Buddhism in India
* systematize Vedānta
* integrate devotion with philosophy
* create pan-Indian monastic institutions

After him, later schools emerged partly in response to Advaita:

* Ramanujacharya → Viśiṣṭādvaita
* Madhvacharya → Dvaita
* Chaitanya Mahaprabhu → Achintya Bhedābheda

So in many ways:

> Śhaṅkara became the central philosophical reference point that later Vedānta schools responded to.

## Traditional View
This mahavakya is often presented as the essence of Advaita Vedanta:
- Brahman alone is satya: Pure awareness, limitless, non-dual.
- World is mithya: Apparent, transactional, dependent on Brahman for existence.
- Jiva is Brahman: The apparent individual is none other than limitless awareness.

## Vedantic Analysis
- World: Neither real nor unreal — a third category, mithya. Like dream, experienced while present, but negated upon enquiry.
- Ignorance: The only “veil” is avidya. Once removed, there is no distance between jiva and Ishvara.
- Resolution: The mahavakya removes the distinction between subject, object, and creator. All are seen as one consciousness.

## Common Misunderstandings
- Mithya = illusion: It does not mean “non-existent.” The world exists for transaction but lacks absolute reality. 
- Jiva disappears: Realization does not annihilate individuality but reveals its dependence and non-separateness.
- Brahman is remote: Brahman is the very Self, not a distant God.
