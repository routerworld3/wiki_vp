The Swaminarayan Sampraday is a prominent Bhakti (devotional) tradition within Hinduism founded by Bhagwan Swaminarayan (1781–1830) in Gujarat, India. Its philosophy seamlessly blends strict moral codes, social reform, and a distinct theological framework.

Part 1: Key Points of Swaminarayan Philosophy
The theological philosophy of Swaminarayan is known as Navya-Vishishtadvaita (Neo-Qualified Non-Dualism). It builds upon the traditional teachings of Ramanujacharya but expands the nature of reality into five eternal, distinct entities:

1. The Five Eternal Realities  
Swaminarayan.org
Unlike traditions that see only one absolute reality, Swaminarayan philosophy dictates that five entities exist eternally:

Jiva (The Soul): Infinite in number, the jiva is the eternal, indestructible consciousness within every living being. It is bound by its own karmas and desires (vasanas).  
Swaminarayan.org

Ishwar (Cosmic Deities): Conscious spiritual beings (such as Brahma, Vishnu, and Shiva) who are far more powerful than jivas and are assigned to govern, create, and sustain individual universes. They, too, are ultimately dependent on God.

Maya: The non-sentient, material energy of God composed of three gunas (sattva, rajas, tamas). It creates the physical universe and causes ignorance by making jivas identify with their physical bodies rather than their eternal souls.

Brahman (or Aksharbrahman): An incredibly vital concept in this tradition. Aksharbrahman is the divine abode of God (Akshardham), but it also manifests as the perfect, ideal devotee. The goal of a spiritual seeker is to become Brahmarupa (pure like Akshar) to perfectly worship God.

Parabrahman (Purushottam): The Supreme Godhead (worshipped as Swaminarayan himself). He is independent, possesses a divine human-like form, governs all other realities, and is the ultimate dispenser of karma.

2. The Concept of Liberation (Moksha)  
Swaminarayan.org
In this philosophy, Moksha does not mean merging into God and losing your identity (like a drop of water into the ocean). Instead, the liberated soul (mukta) gains a divine body, travels to the supreme abode (Akshardham), and eternally serves and enjoys the bliss of Parabrahman. The soul retains its individuality but exists in a permanent master-servant relationship (Swami-sevak bhava) with God.

Part 2: The Reason for Gender Segregation
One of the most noticeable features of the Swaminarayan tradition (especially in orthodox sub-sects like BAPS) is the strict institutional segregation of men and women. Men and women sit on separate sides during temple services, host separate assemblies, and women do not interact directly with the celibate male monks (sadhus or swamis).  
Pluralism Project Archive - Harvard University

The reasons for this setup are deeply tied to both historical context and specific spiritual vows:

1. Strict Ascetic Vows (Nishkam Vrat)
The monastic order (sadhus) in the Swaminarayan tradition takes exceptionally rigid vows of lifelong celibacy, known as Nishkam (non-lust). To protect the purity of these vows and prevent any mental or physical distractions, the sadhus maintain complete detachment from women. They do not look at, speak to, or have any physical proximity to women. The institutional segregation ensures that monks can fulfill their monastic duties without breaking their spiritual vows.  
The Metrognome

2. Historical Context and Safety of Women
When Bhagwan Swaminarayan founded the sect in early 19th-century Gujarat, the region was socially turbulent. Women faced severe exploitation, and several contemporary religious cults practiced hedonistic rituals where women were subjected to sexual abuse under the guise of religion.

By enforcing strict segregation in religious spaces, Swaminarayan provided a safe, secure environment where women could worship without fearing harassment or the predatory behavior of men.  
Swaminarayan.org

3. Empowerment through Autonomy
Historically, separating the genders actually allowed women to take complete ownership of their spiritual lives. Because men were prohibited from entering women's spaces, the tradition established a parallel wing managed entirely by women (known as Samkhyayoginis or lay female leaders).  
Swaminarayan.org

Within these segregated spaces, women preach to other women, manage funds, organize events, and lead services without male interference or oversight.  
Swaminarayan.org

Furthermore, Bhagwan Swaminarayan was a fierce advocate for women's rights in his time: he actively fought against female infanticide (drowning newborn girls in milk), campaigned to abolish Sati (widow burning), and championed female education.  
Swaminarayan.org

How it is Viewed Today
While critics view this practice as outdated or unequal, apologists and female devotees within the tradition argue that the soul (Atman) has no gender. They see the physical separation not as a sign of spiritual inferiority, but as a mutual boundary of respect that fosters focus during worship and grants women an independent, autonomous space to thrive
